Tata Motors Stock Analysis (1995–2025)

This project was a deep dive into the financial and market performance of Tata Motors over three decades, from 1995 to 2025. The dataset was extensive, containing more than 7,800 rows and 15 columns sourced from the National Stock Exchange (NSE). It included daily trading data such as open, high, low, close prices, trading volumes, turnover, VWAP (Volume Weighted Average Price), and moving averages.

The first stage was data cleaning and preparation. Raw financial datasets often contain missing values, inconsistencies, or irrelevant fields. I had to carefully inspect the dataset, remove anomalies, and standardize formats. This process taught me the importance of precision in data preparation — even small errors can distort long‑term trends. Once the dataset was ready, I moved into exploratory data analysis (EDA).

I plotted the close price trend across thirty years, which revealed cyclical growth patterns. Tata Motors experienced strong rallies in the mid‑2000s, corrections around 2010, and a remarkable recovery post‑2020, peaking near 2024. Trading volumes surged significantly after 2016, with an exceptional peak in 2021. Risk analysis showed most daily returns clustered near zero, but extreme outliers — including a shocking –80% return — revealed significant tail risks. Moving averages highlighted stair‑step growth and cyclical volatility.

A particularly interesting part of the project was the VWAP vs Close Price analysis. By plotting these values, I discovered a strong linear correlation, confirming that VWAP is a reliable predictor of closing prices. Beyond the technical analysis, the project also involved creating a polished presentation and speech. Working with my team — Aryan, Vaibhav, and myself — we structured the presentation to highlight key insights and delivered it in front of an audience.

In conclusion, the Tata Motors Stock Analysis Project demonstrated the company’s long‑term growth potential while underscoring the volatility inherent in the automotive sector. It taught me valuable lessons about data cleaning, exploratory analysis, risk evaluation, and effective communication. More importantly, it showcased how data science can bridge the gap between raw numbers and strategic decision‑making.

AdventureWorks Power BI Dashboard

Inspired by the Microsoft Power BI course on Udemy, I built a full‑scale interactive dashboard using the AdventureWorks dataset. The project began with data modeling — creating relationships between tables, defining measures with DAX, and ensuring the schema supported advanced analysis. I built calculated columns to segment customers by purchasing behavior and measures to calculate year‑over‑year growth.

Next, I designed multiple reports tailored to different audiences. The Executive Summary Dashboard highlighted KPIs such as total revenue, profit margins, and customer growth. The Customer‑Product Analysis Dashboard focused on sales performance at a granular level, showing top‑selling items, customer segments, and purchasing trends. The Continental Report visualized sales distribution across regions, using maps and bar charts to show geographic performance.

To enhance usability, I created custom icons and an interactive navigation pane. Instead of scrolling through multiple pages, users could click icons to jump directly to the dashboard they needed. I also applied consistent color themes and formatting to ensure clarity and visual appeal. Performance optimization was another challenge — I used techniques like aggregations, query folding, and efficient DAX formulas.

The final dashboard was not just a technical achievement but also a communication tool. I presented it to peers, explaining how each report served a specific business need. The feedback was overwhelmingly positive — users found the dashboard intuitive, insightful, and visually engaging. This project taught me how to combine technical Power BI skills with design thinking, showing that business intelligence is about telling a story with data.

Perfume Analytics Project

The Perfume Analytics Project was a fascinating exploration of consumer behavior in a niche industry. Unlike stock analysis or corporate dashboards, this project focused on understanding how people choose fragrances — a decision that is deeply personal yet influenced by broader market trends.

I began with a raw dataset containing customer purchase records, fragrance categories, and demographic information. The first step was data cleaning: removing duplicates, handling missing values, and standardizing product categories. Once the dataset was clean, I moved into exploratory data analysis (EDA).

The EDA revealed intriguing patterns. Certain fragrances were more popular in specific seasons — floral scents peaked in spring, while woody and musky fragrances saw higher sales in winter. Demographic analysis showed younger customers tended to experiment with bold, unconventional scents, while older demographics preferred classic fragrances. Visualization was key: heatmaps and scatterplots highlighted trends that had practical implications for marketing strategies.

Beyond the technical analysis, this project taught me about the intersection of data science and consumer psychology. Fragrance is an emotional product — people choose scents that reflect their identity, mood, or aspirations. By analyzing data, I uncovered the hidden logic behind these emotional decisions, reinforcing my belief that data science is about understanding human behavior.

Capstone Project – End‑to‑End Data Pipeline

My Capstone Project is the most ambitious undertaking of my academic journey. It integrates multiple technologies into a single, end‑to‑end solution that demonstrates my ability to handle every stage of the data lifecycle.

The project begins with data ingestion, where raw data is collected from multiple sources — SQL databases, APIs, and CSV files. I built scripts to automate this process. Next comes data cleaning and transformation, where I apply Python libraries like Pandas and NumPy to standardize formats and prepare datasets for analysis.

The heart of the project is machine learning modeling. Using Scikit‑Learn, I developed models to predict outcomes based on historical data. I experimented with different algorithms — logistic regression, decision trees, and random forests — comparing their accuracy and performance. Hyperparameter tuning and cross‑validation ensured robustness and reliability. This stage taught me how to balance accuracy with interpretability, ensuring that models were not only powerful but also explainable to stakeholders.

Once the models were built, I focused on deployment. Using Docker, I containerized the application, making it portable and easy to run across different environments. I also explored cloud deployment options, considering platforms like AWS and Azure. This step was crucial because it transformed the project from a local experiment into a scalable solution that could be used in real‑world scenarios.

Visualization was another key component. I built dashboards in Power BI to present model outputs, KPIs, and trends. These dashboards were designed for non‑technical stakeholders, ensuring that insights were accessible and actionable. To make the project more interactive, I integrated a web front‑end using React and Node.js, allowing users to input data and view predictions in real time. This combination of machine learning and web development created a seamless user experience.

Finally, I explored Web3 concepts, experimenting with decentralized applications and blockchain integration. While this was more exploratory than practical, it gave me a glimpse into the future of data systems, where decentralization and transparency could play a major role. This exploration broadened my perspective and encouraged me to think about how emerging technologies might reshape the way we build and deploy data solutions.

The Capstone Project represents the culmination of my learning journey. It combines everything I’ve studied — SQL, Python, Power BI, web development, machine learning, and even emerging technologies. More importantly, it demonstrates my ability to think holistically, designing solutions that are not only technically sound but also user‑friendly and scalable. This project is not just a portfolio piece; it is a statement of my ambition to become a full‑stack developer with strong data science expertise.
